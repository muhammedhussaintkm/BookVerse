				\textbf{ABSTRACT}\\
			\end{large}
		\end{center}
	
	   	
	
Vedic Mathematics is an ancient system of Indian mathematics rediscovered from the Vedas, particularly from the Atharva Veda. It offers simplified and efficient techniques for mathematical calculations. Among its powerful applications is the rapid multiplication of large numbers. This paper delves into the application of Vedic sutras—especially the Urdhva Tiryakbhyam Sutra—to perform 4x4 digit multiplication with remarkable speed and simplicity compared to conventional methods.

The Urdhva Tiryakbhyam Sutra, meaning "Vertically and Crosswise", is a general multiplication formula applicable to all cases of multiplication. In contrast to the traditional long multiplication, which becomes increasingly cumbersome as the number of digits grows, the Vedic method breaks down the multiplication into a series of logical, manageable steps. This allows both mental and written computation to be carried out with greater efficiency, reducing the time and potential for error, which is especially beneficial in competitive exams, coding, electronics, and VLSI implementation.

This abstract elaborates on the principles of Vedic multiplication for 4-digit numbers, offers step-by-step examples, compares performance with traditional algorithms, highlights benefits in computational applications, and proposes its inclusion in modern-day educational and technical practices.

		\chapter{INTRODUCTION}
		\begin{onehalfspace}
			
	
		Multiplication is an important fundamental function in arithmetic operations. Multiplication-based operations such as Multiply and Accumulate(MAC) and inner product are among some of the frequently used Computation- Intensive Arithmetic Functions(CIAF) currently implemented in many Digital Signal Processing (DSP) applications such as convolution, Fast Fourier Transform(FFT), filtering and in microprocessors in its arithmetic and logic unit. Since multiplication dominates the execution time of most DSP algorithms, so there is a need of high speed multiplier. Currently, multiplication time is still the dominant factor in determining the instruction cycle time of a DSP chip.

The demand for high speed processing has been increasing as a result of expanding computer and signal processing applications. Higher throughput arithmetic operations are important to achieve the desired performance in many real-time signal and image processing applications. One of the key arithmetic operations in such applications is multiplication and the development of fast multiplier circuit has been a subject of interest over decades. Reducing the time delay and power consumption are very essential requirements for many applications. This work presents different multiplier architectures. Multiplier based on Vedic Mathematics is one of the fast and low power multiplier.

Minimizing power consumption for digital systems involves optimization at all levels of the design. This optimization includes the technology used to implement the digital circuits, the circuit style and topology, the architecture for implementing the circuits and at the highest level the algorithms that are being implemented. Digital multipliers are the most commonly used components in any digital circuit design. They are fast, reliable and efficient components that are utilized to implement any operation. Depending upon the arrangement of the components, there are different types of multipliers available. Particular multiplier architecture is chosen based on the application.

In many DSP algorithms, the multiplier lies in the critical delay path and ultimately determines the performance of algorithm. The speed of multiplication operation is of great importance in DSP as well as in general processor. In the past multiplication was implemented generally with a sequence of addition, subtraction and 
Shift operations. There have been many algorithms proposals in literature to perform multiplication, each offering different advantages and having trade off in terms of speed, circuit complexity, and area and power consumption.

For multiplication algorithms performed in DSP applications latency and throughput are the two major concerns from delay perspective. Latency is the real delay of computing a function, a measure of how long the inputs to a device are stable is the final result available on outputs. Throughput is the measure of how many multiplications can be performed in a given period of time; multiplier is not only a high delay block but also a major source of power dissipation. That’s why if one also aims to minimize power consumption, it is of great interest to reduce the delay by using various delay optimizations.

Digital multipliers are the core components of all the digital signal processors (DSPs) and the speed of the DSP is largely determined by the speed of its multipliers. Two most common multiplication algorithms followed in the digital hardware are array multiplication algorithm and Booth multiplication algorithm. The computation time taken by the array multiplier is comparatively less because the partial products are calculated independently in parallel. The delay associated with the array multiplier is the time taken by the signals to propagate through the gates that form the multiplication array. Booth multiplication is another important multiplication algorithm. Large booth arrays are required for high speed multiplication and exponential operations which in turn require large partial sum and partial carry registers. Multiplication of two n-bit operands using a radix-4 booth recording multiplier requires approximately n / (2m) clock cycles to generate the least significant half of the final product, where m is the number of Booth recorder adder stages. Thus, a large propagation delay is associated with this case. Due to the importance of digital multipliers in DSP, it has always been an active area of research and a number of interesting multiplication algorithms have been reported in the literature.

In this thesis work, Urdhva tiryakbhyam Sutra is first applied to the binary number system and is used to develop digital multiplier architecture. This is shown to be very similar to the popular array multiplier architecture. This Sutra also shows the effectiveness of to reduce the NXN multiplier structure into an efficient 4X4 multiplier structures.
Nikhilam Sutra is then discussed and is shown to be much more efficient in the multiplication of large numbers as it reduces the multiplication of two large numbers to that of two smaller ones. The proposed multiplication algorithm is then illustrated to show its computational efficiency by taking an example of reducing a 4X4-bit multiplication to a single 2X2-bit multiplication operation. This work presents a systematic design methodology for fast and area efficient digit multiplier based on Vedic mathematics. The Multiplier Architecture is based on the Vertical and Crosswise algorithm of ancient Indian Vedic Mathematics.                                                                 .

    \section{History of Vedic Mathematics:-}

Vedic mathematics is part of four Vedas (books of wisdom). It is part of Sthapatya- Veda (book on civil engineering and architecture), which is an upa-veda (supplement) of Atharva Veda. It covers explanation of several modern mathematical terms including arithmetic, geometry (plane, co-ordinate), trigonometry, quadratic equations, factorization and even calculus.

His Holiness Jagadguru Shankaracharya Bharati Krishna Teerthaji Maharaja (1884-1960) comprised all this work together and gave its mathematical explanation while discussing it for various applications. Swamiji constructed 16 sutras (formulae) and 16 Upa sutras (sub formulae) after extensive research in Atharva Veda. Obviously these formulae are not to be found in present text of Atharva Veda because these formulae were constructed by Swamiji himself. Vedic mathematics is not only a mathematical wonder but also it is logical. That’s why VM has such a degree of eminence which cannot be disapproved. Due these phenomenal characteristic, VM has already crossed the boundaries of India and has become a leading topic of research abroad. VM deals with several basic as well as complex mathematical operations. Especially, methods of basic arithmetic are extremely simple and powerful.

The word ‘Vedic’ is derived from the word ‘Veda’ which means the store-house of all knowledge. Vedic mathematics is mainly based on 16 Sutras (or aphorisms) dealing with various branches of mathematics like arithmetic, algebra, geometry etc. These Sutras along with their brief meanings are enlisted below alphabetically.

1)	(Anurupye) Shunyamanyat – If one is in ratio, the other is zero. 

2)	Chalana-Kalanabyham – Differences and Similarities. 

3)	Ekadhikina Purvena – By one more than the previous One. 

4)	Ekanyunena Purvena – By one less than the previous one. 

5)	Gunakasamuchyah – The factors of the sum is equal to the sum of the factors. 

6)	Gunitasamuchyah – The product of the sum is equal to the sum of the product. 

7)	Nikhilam Navatashcaramam Dashatah – All from 9 and last from 10. 
8)	Paraavartya Yojayet – Transpose and adjust. 

9)	Puranapuranabyham – By the completion or noncompletion. 

10)	Sankalana- vyavakalanabhyam – By addition and by subtraction. 

11)	Shesanyankena Charamena – The remainders by the last digit. 

12)	Shunyam Saamyasamuccaye – When the sum is the same that sum is zero. 

13)	Sopaantyadvayamantyam – The ultimate and twice the penultimate. 

14)	Urdhva-tiryakbhyam – Vertically and crosswise. 

15)	Vyashtisamanstih – Part and Whole. 

16)	Yaavadunam – Whatever the extent of its deficiency. 

These methods and ideas can be directly applied to trigonometry, plain and spherical geometry, conics, calculus (both differential and integral), and applied mathematics of various kinds. As mentioned earlier, all these Sutras were reconstructed from ancient Vedic texts early in the last century. Many Sub-sutras were also discovered at the same time, which are not discussed here.

The beauty of Vedic mathematics lies in the fact that it reduces the otherwise cumbersome-looking calculations in conventional mathematics to a very simple one. This is so because the Vedic formulae are claimed to be based on the natural principles on which the human mind works. This is a very interesting field and presents some effective algorithms which can be applied to various branches of engineering such as computing and digital signal processing.

The multiplier architecture can be generally classified into three categories. First is the serial multiplier which emphasizes on hardware and minimum amount of chip area. Second is parallel multiplier (array and tree) which carries out high speed mathematical operations. But the drawback is the relatively larger chip area consumption. Third is serial- parallel multiplier which serves as a good trade-off between the times consuming serial multiplier and the area consuming parallel multipliers.
\section{2.2 ALGORITHMS OF VEDIC MATHEMATICS:-}

2.2.1 Vedic Multiplication

The proposed Vedic multiplier is based on the Vedic multiplication formulae (Sutras). These Sutras have been traditionally used for the multiplication of two numbers in the decimal number system. In this work, we apply the same ideas to the binary number system to make the proposed algorithm compatible with the digital hardware. Vedic multiplication based on some algorithms, some are discussed below

2.2.1.1 Urdhva Tiryakbhyam sutra

The multiplier is based on an algorithm Urdhva Tiryakbhyam (Vertical & Crosswise) of ancient Indian Vedic Mathematics. Urdhva Tiryakbhyam Sutra is a general multiplication formula applicable to all cases of multiplication. It literally means

“Vertically and crosswise” It is based on a novel concept through which the generation of all partial products can be done with the concurrent addition of these partial products. The parallelism in generation of partial products and their summation is obtained using Urdhava Triyakbhyam explained in fig 2.1. The algorithm can be generalized for n x n bit number. Since the partial products and their sums are calculated in parallel, the multiplier is independent of the clock frequency of the processor. Thus the multiplier will require the same amount of time to calculate the product and hence is independent of the clock frequency. The net advantage is that it reduces the need of microprocessors to operate at increasingly high clock frequencies. While a higher clock frequency generally results in increased processing power, its disadvantage is that it also increases power dissipation which results in higher device operating temperatures. By adopting the Vedic multiplier, microprocessors designers can easily circumvent these problems to avoid catastrophic device failures. Due to its regular structure, it can be easily layout in a silicon chip. The Multiplier has the advantage that as the number of bits increases, gate delay and area increases very slowly as compared to other multipliers.

1) Multiplication of two decimal numbers- 325*738

To illustrate this multiplication scheme, let us consider the multiplication of two decimal numbers (325 * 738). Line diagram for the multiplication is shown in Fig.2.1. The digits on the both sides of the line are multiplied and added with the carry from the previous step. This generates one of the bits of the result and a carry. This carry is added in the next step and hence the process goes on. If more than one line are there in one step, all the results are added to the previous carry. In each step, least significant bit acts as the result bit and all other bits act as carry for the next step, initially the carry is taken to be zero.

 3.1 Mathematical Formulation of Vedic Sutras
The gifts of the ancient Indian mathematics in the world history of mathematical science are not well recognized. 3.1.1 Urdhva-tiryakbyham
To illustrate the multiplication algorithm using Urdhva Tiryakbyham, let us consider the multiplication of two binary numbers a3a2a1a0 and b3b2b1b0. As the result of this multiplication would be more than 4 bits, we express it as ........R3R2R1R0. Line diagram for multiplication of two 4-bit numbers is shown in Fig. 3.1 which is nothing but the mapping of the Fig.2.1 in binary system. For the simplicity, each bit is represented by a circle. Least significant bit R0 is obtained by multiplying the least significant bits of the multiplicand and the multiplier. The process is followed according to the steps shown in Fig. 3.1. The hardware realization of a 4-bit multiplier is shown in figure3.2.

Figure 3.1: Line diagram for multiplication of two 4 - bit numbers.

Firstly,  least  significant  bits  are  multiplied  which  gives  the  least  significant  bit  of  the product (vertical). Then, the LSB of the multiplicand is multiplied with the next higher bit of  the  multiplier  and  added  with  the  product  of  LSB  of  multiplier  and  next  higher  bit  of the  multiplicand  (crosswise).  The  sum  gives  second  bit  of  the  product  and  the  carry  is added   in   the   output   of   next   stage   sum   obtained   by   the   crosswise   and   vertical multiplication   and   addition   of   three   bits   of   the   two   numbers   from   least   significant position.  Next,  all  the  four  bits  are  processed  with  crosswise  multiplication  and  addition to give the sum and carry. The sum corresponding bit of the product and the carry is 10 again added to the next stage multiplication and addition of three bits except the LSB. The same operation continues until the multiplication of the two MSBs to give the MSB of the product. For example, if in some intermediate step, we get 110, then 0 will act as result bit (referred as rn) and 11 as the carry (referred as ‘cn’). It should be clearly noted that ‘cn’ may be a multi-bit number.


With C6R6R5R4R3R2R1R0 being the final product. Hence this is the general mathematical formula applicable to all cases of multiplication.

const int irSensorPin = 2;
int sensorState = 0;

void setup() {
  pinMode(irSensorPin, INPUT);      // Set IR sensor pin as input
  Serial.begin(9600);               // Start serial communication at 9600 bps
}

void loop() {
  sensorState = digitalRead(irSensorPin); // Read sensor output
  
  if (sensorState == LOW) {
    // Object detected (most IR sensors output LOW when triggered)
    Serial.println("Movement Detected");
  } else {
    // No object
    Serial.println("No Movement");
  }

  delay(1000); // 1-second delay between readings

	  			The working of this project, titled “Suspicious Activity Detection Using IR Sensor and Python,” is centered on the minimalist yet powerful integration of an IR sensor, an Arduino UNO, and a Python script using the yagmail library to send email alerts. This system is designed to monitor for suspicious physical movement, detect it using a reflective infrared sensor, and immediately notify the user via email, all while using only essential components without any camera or internet-connected microcontrollers. The elegance of this setup lies in its simplicity, affordability, and efficient operation that can serve as a foundational model for home or office intrusion detection systems.

The project begins with the IR sensor, a simple but effective component that works on the principle of emitting infrared radiation and detecting the reflection from nearby surfaces. This sensor is not a passive infrared sensor that detects heat, but rather a reflective IR module consisting of an infrared LED and a phototransistor. The LED continuously emits infrared light, which reflects off any object that comes within the detection range. When a reflective surface, such as a human hand, a body, or any object with decent reflectivity enters the field, the reflected IR light hits the phototransistor, changing its conductivity. This alteration causes the sensor’s digital output pin to send a LOW signal to the Arduino, signifying that an object has been detected. When there is no object in range, the sensor sends a HIGH signal. This binary behavior makes it extremely easy to interface with the Arduino UNO, which reads this HIGH/LOW state from a digital input pin, typically pin 2 or 3.

The Arduino UNO, based on the ATmega328P microcontroller, serves as the real-time processor that constantly reads the signal from the IR sensor. The microcontroller code is written in Arduino C/C++ and uploaded using the Arduino IDE. In the setup, the IR sensor’s digital OUT pin is connected to one of the Arduino’s digital input pins, while the sensor’s VCC and GND are connected to the Arduino’s 5V and GND pins respectively. The code on the Arduino sets up the pin mode for input and opens a serial connection using Serial.begin(9600). The loop() function continuously reads the sensor’s state using digitalRead() and checks whether the value is HIGH or LOW. When the value turns LOW, the Arduino interprets it as motion or intrusion detection and immediately sends a specific message over the serial port, such as "Motion Detected" or "Intruder Alert". This message is sent using the Serial.println() function, which writes data to the Arduino’s serial interface over USB.

This USB serial communication forms the backbone of data transfer between the Arduino and the Python script running on a computer. Unlike network-based systems, this project leverages the reliable USB connection for power and data exchange. The serial data sent from Arduino is read by the computer using a Python script that listens to the correct COM port. Here, the role of Python becomes critical. The script uses the pyserial module to open the serial port, read incoming messages, and parse them in real time. It continuously checks for new lines using ser.readline() in a loop and decodes them into readable strings. Once the string “Motion Detected” is identified, the Python script triggers an alert mechanism by calling a function to send an email using the yagmail library.

The yagmail library is a Python module specifically designed for simplifying the process of sending emails through Gmail. Unlike smtplib, which requires manual handling of SMTP authentication and email formatting, yagmail abstracts these complexities and offers a more user-friendly API. In the script, the user first logs into Gmail using their credentials (ideally stored securely or passed via an app password or environment variable). Then, a new email is composed and sent using yag.send(). The subject might be something like “Intrusion Alert Detected!”, and the body can include dynamic content such as the time and date of the event. The beauty of using yagmail is that it makes it possible to send a fully functioning email alert in just a few lines of code, making it ideal for lightweight microcontroller-based systems like this.

To ensure the system runs continuously, the Python script runs in an infinite loop, waiting for serial input. Whenever motion is detected, the Arduino sends a signal over serial, and the Python program instantly responds. The script can be further enhanced to include a delay or cooldown timer, so repeated motions within a short period do not trigger multiple emails. Alternatively, a logging mechanism can be added to record the time and frequency of detections. This allows for future analysis of the data, such as determining whether certain times of day experience more activity. For safety and usability, error handling is implemented in the Python code to detect and recover from serial disconnections or failed email attempts. Exception blocks capture these cases and either attempt to reconnect or safely exit the loop.

This minimal hardware approach ensures low energy consumption, small form factor, and high portability. The Arduino is powered entirely by the USB port of the computer, eliminating the need for an external power source. The IR sensor, drawing only a few milliamps of current, can run indefinitely under such a setup. The hardware wiring is also extremely simple: only three connections (VCC, GND, and OUT) are required from the IR sensor to the Arduino. This makes the system highly reproducible and suitable for rapid prototyping or educational demonstrations.

In operation, the system can be mounted in doorways, windows, corridors, or any point of entry to detect unexpected activity. For best results, the IR sensor should be placed facing downward at an angle or horizontally across a hallway to cover a broader field of reflection. Ambient lighting and surface reflectivity can influence sensor accuracy, so testing and calibration may be required to ensure reliable detection. For example, darker objects may reflect less infrared light and could result in weaker detection signals. Adjusting sensor sensitivity, if supported by onboard potentiometers, can fine-tune the response threshold.

This project also emphasizes data privacy and efficiency. Since no camera is used, there is no risk of visual data leaks, making this system privacy-respecting and compliant for indoor use in bedrooms, offices, or even bathrooms where surveillance is not acceptable. Furthermore, the system doesn’t require constant internet connection. As long as the computer running Python has access to Gmail’s SMTP server (i.e., has internet access), it can send emails, even from a remote or isolated room.

Testing the system involves verifying each module independently. Initially, the IR sensor’s output is monitored directly by connecting its output to an LED or multimeter. The Arduino is then tested using Serial Monitor to ensure it properly receives and prints sensor state changes. Once verified, the Python script is run to confirm it reads the Arduino’s serial output accurately and successfully sends an email. The reliability of yagmail and Gmail servers means email delivery is usually instant, with messages appearing in the user’s inbox within seconds of detection.

In essence, the complete working process can be described as a continuous feedback loop: The IR sensor detects physical presence and signals the Arduino; the Arduino processes this and sends a message over USB; the Python script captures this and instantly sends an email alert via yagmail. This minimalist pipeline ensures maximum effectiveness with minimum resource usage. The real-time aspect of the system ensures that alerts are not delayed, making it ideal for immediate notification in case of intrusions.

Overall, the system demonstrates how a basic sensor, an Arduino UNO, and a Python script can work together to form an effective real-world security solution without any fancy or costly hardware. It combines basic electronics with real-time software integration to provide a fast, reliable, and practical method for detecting unauthorized presence. By focusing on minimal components and using yagmail for email integration, the system becomes easy to maintain, modify, and expand. In the future, this working model can serve as the basis for further developments, such as integrating with IoT dashboards, enabling mobile alerts, or adding additional sensors for extended coverage, all while preserving its low-cost and efficient roots

      \section{Ir sensor}
     An Infrared (IR) sensor is an electronic device that detects infrared radiation (IR light) emitted or reflected by objects in its surroundings. In the context of this project—“Suspicious Activity Detection Using IR Sensor and Python”—a reflective IR sensor is used to detect the presence or motion of nearby objects without physical contact. This type of IR sensor is commonly used in automation and detection systems due to its simplicity, low cost, and ease of integration with microcontrollers like the Arduino UNO.

The reflective IR sensor typically consists of two main components: an IR emitter (usually an IR LED) and a receiver (photodiode or phototransistor). The emitter continuously emits infrared light. When an object comes close to the sensor, the emitted IR rays are reflected back and received by the photodiode. The amount of reflected IR light depends on the distance and the reflectivity of the object. Based on the reflected IR intensity, the sensor changes its output voltage, which can be read as a digital HIGH or LOW signal.

In a typical reflective IR sensor, the output is LOW when an object is detected (because reflected IR light is received), and HIGH when there is no object present (no reflection). This binary nature makes it extremely easy to use with digital input pins on microcontrollers. For example, the IR sensor’s output pin can be connected to a digital input on the Arduino UNO, and the program can use digitalRead() to check whether motion or presence is detected.

One of the key advantages of reflective IR sensors is their compact design and fast response time. They do not require complex image processing or environmental learning, making them ideal for simple object detection applications. Additionally, they consume very low power, often operating directly from a 5V supply provided by the Arduino UNO itself. The sensor can be positioned at doorways, hallways, or entry points to detect movement across its sensing path.

However, reflective IR sensors also have some limitations. Their detection accuracy depends on ambient lighting conditions, surface color, and object reflectivity. Dark or absorbent surfaces may reflect less IR light, reducing detection efficiency. Similarly, strong sunlight or nearby IR sources can interfere with the sensor’s readings. Despite these limitations, for indoor applications such as motion or intrusion detection, they are highly effective when placed properly and calibrated for the environment

	6.	Send Email: The script then sends an email using yagmail to notify the user.

The accuracy and sensitivity of the IR sensor heavily depend on the surface properties of the object. Bright and shiny objects are easily detected due to strong IR reflection, whereas dark, matte, or non-reflective surfaces (like black clothing or soft fabric) may not reflect enough infrared light for reliable detection, leading to missed alerts.
\item False Positives in Certain Conditions

Sudden changes in ambient lighting (such as sunlight entering through a window) or nearby reflective surfaces (like glass or mirrors) can cause false triggers. This might result in unnecessary email alerts being sent, which could become bothersome or lead to desensitization to alerts.

	•	Limited Range: The IR sensor is only effective within a small range, making it suitable for short-distance detection only.\\
	•	Line of Sight Requirement: It cannot detect motion around corners or in blind spots\\.
	•	No Visual Feedback: Unlike camera-based systems, it doesn’t provide a visual record of the event.\\
	•	Single Channel Alerting: Email is the only alert medium in the current version.\\
	•	Internet Dependency: Without an internet connection, the Python script cannot send alerts, which could render the system ineffective in network outages.\\

These limitations provide a clear path for future development and improvement.\\

Vision for Future Enhancement\\

The conclusion of this project also marks the beginning of potential enhancements. There is scope to integrate the system with:\\
	•	Wi-Fi or GSM modules for wireless alerts.\\
	•	Cameras to capture images or stream video\\.
	•	Cloud platforms for data logging and remote access.\\
	•	AI-based models to differentiate between real threats and false alarms.\\
	•	Mobile app integration for user-friendly control and real-time alerts.\\

By integrating these technologies, the system can evolve from a basic prototype into a smart surveillance product ready for commercial deployment.\\

Final Thoughts\\

To conclude, the “Suspicious Activity Detection Using IR Sensor and Python” project is a successful proof-of-concept that demonstrates how minimal hardware can deliver impactful results when combined with intelligent software solutions. It represents a practical response to growing security concerns in homes and small businesses, offering a functional, scalable, and affordable solution.\\

The knowledge and experience gained through this project pave the way for more complex and robust systems in the future. It encourages innovation, promotes the use of open-source technologies, and proves that simplicity can often be the foundation for powerful ideas.\\

The project serves as a reminder that impactful technology doesn’t always require complex components—sometimes, a humble IR sensor, a microcontroller, and a few lines of Python are all it takes to build something useful, meaningful, and potentially life-saving.
